In Cairns, the difference between hitting competent rock and finding wet, weathered clay at six metres can change an entire foundation design. We see it often on sites around the Barron River floodplain and up toward the Kuranda range foothills. Heavy seasonal rain saturates the upper layers, making seismic methods tricky, which is where a resistivity survey becomes useful. Vertical Electrical Sounding gives us a continuous vertical profile of apparent resistivity, revealing transitions from alluvial sand to residual granite or mudstone that would be invisible from the surface. We combine this with targeted test pitting when the top two metres need direct visual confirmation of fill or organics before the deep resistivity interpretation begins.

Service characteristics in Cairns

A recent project near the Cairns Esplanade required depth-to-bedrock confirmation under a proposed mid-rise. The developer had preliminary borehole data showing weathered metamorphics at nine metres, but the site varied laterally—some zones dropped to fourteen metres in the VES profiles. We ran a Schlumberger array with maximum AB/2 spacing of 100 metres, resolving layers down to roughly 25 metres. The resistivity contrast between the conductive marine clay and the resistive fresh rock was sharp enough that we could map the bedrock surface across the entire footprint in a single afternoon. For sites in the Redlynch Valley where the water table fluctuates seasonally, we often run a CPT test alongside VES to calibrate resistivity against tip resistance and sleeve friction—this dual approach reduces ambiguity when interpreting intermediate-resistivity zones that might be either dense sand or lightly cemented gravel.

Typical technical challenges in Cairns

Cairns sits at roughly 16.9° south, right in the wet tropics where annual rainfall exceeds two metres. That changes the electrical game entirely. Dry-season resistivity contrasts can blur or reverse in February after a monsoonal trough parks over the city for a week. A clay layer that reads 15 Ω·m in July might drop below 8 Ω·m when fully saturated, overlapping with saline groundwater signatures. Misreading that shift can lead to overestimating bedrock depth or misidentifying a weathered zone as competent rock. We run VES surveys across multiple seasons when the budget allows, and always cross-reference with at least one borehole or dynamic probe log. The Cyclone Jasper floods of 2023 reinforced something every local engineering geologist already knew: subsurface assumptions built on a single dry-season dataset are risky here.

Our services

Our electrical resistivity work in Far North Queensland covers two main approaches, both integrated with conventional drilling data to reduce interpretation uncertainty.

VES Depth Sounding

Schlumberger array vertical soundings for mapping depth to bedrock, detecting buried paleochannels, and profiling groundwater salinity in alluvial environments around the Cairns region. Each sounding point produces a 1D resistivity model tied to local geology.

2D Resistivity Imaging

Multi-electrode Wenner or dipole-dipole arrays for continuous cross-sections where lateral changes matter—karst features in limestone near Chillagoe, cavity detection under proposed embankments, or delineating fresh-saltwater interfaces near the coast.

Q&A

How deep can a VES survey reach in the Cairns area?

With a maximum AB/2 electrode spacing of 100 to 150 metres, we typically resolve layers down to 25–40 metres in the alluvial and residual soils common around the Cairns coastal plain. Deeper penetration is possible with longer spreads, but signal strength drops and cultural noise from nearby infrastructure becomes a limiting factor. We discuss depth requirements during project scoping and adjust the array geometry accordingly.

What does a resistivity survey cost for a typical residential or small commercial site in Cairns?

For a single VES sounding point with full processing, interpretation, and a report tied to AS 1726, budget between AU$1,020 and AU$1,810 depending on access conditions, maximum depth required, and whether we need supplementary data like a nearby borehole log for calibration. Multiple sounding points or a 2D line reduce the per-point cost.

Can electrical resistivity work during the wet season in Cairns?

Yes, and in some cases the saturated ground actually improves electrode coupling and reduces contact resistance. The main challenge is interpretation: saturated clays and saline groundwater produce overlapping resistivity ranges, so we rely more heavily on borehole calibration during the wet months. We also avoid surveys during active heavy rain because surface water can short electrodes and distort readings.
